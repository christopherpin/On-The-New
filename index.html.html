Beyond the Plaque is a proposed living archive of New York City's architecture — designated and undesignated alike. It documents the cornices, ironwork, and craft of buildings that fall outside a slow, selective, and necessarily limited legal process.

Case file

How a building earns its plaque

New York's Landmarks Preservation Commission follows a deliberate, six-stage legal process. It is thorough by design — and, by design, only a small fraction of the city's buildings ever enter it.

01 · SURVEY

Research staff identify candidates

LPC's Research Department surveys neighborhoods and building types to flag structures of potential historic, architectural, or cultural merit.

01
02 · EVALUATE

Merit is weighed against priorities

Staff assess a candidate against agency priorities, the goal of representing all five boroughs, and how it compares to resources already designated.

02
03 · CALENDAR

The full Commission votes to hear it

A majority of Commissioners present must vote to "calendar" the building — the first formal step, and by no means guaranteed.

03
04 · HEARING

A report is written, testimony is heard

Staff draft a detailed report; owners receive a copy in advance. At a public hearing, anyone may testify for or against designation.

04
05 · VOTE

Six commissioners must say yes

A majority of the eleven-member Commission — six votes — is required. Designation takes effect immediately upon that vote.

05
06 · FILE

City Planning and Council review

The City Planning Commission has 60 days to weigh zoning impact; the City Council can still modify or disapprove within 120 days.

06

A rigorous process, built for permanence — which is exactly why it moves slowly, and why so much of the city's ordinary architecture never reaches step one.

The proposal

Designation isn't the only kind of memory

DESIGNATED, RECORDED SURVEYED, WAITING NEVER SURVEYED

Legal designation was never meant to be a complete record — it's a protective status, reserved for buildings that clear a high, deliberately narrow bar. Most of the city's architecture, including plenty of it worth remembering, will never clear that bar and was never surveyed in the first place.

Beyond the Plaque proposes an open, ongoing archive that runs alongside the LPC process rather than in competition with it — documenting the cornice details, ironwork, materials, and human stories of buildings whether or not they ever reach a Commission vote.

Document

Conservator-grade photography, materials notes, and measured drawings for buildings outside the designation pipeline.

Contribute

A public submission tool so residents can record their own building's features and history before they're altered or lost.

Connect

Every entry cross-referenced to LPC survey and designation data, so the archive shows exactly where the legal record ends.

Public-facing tools for residents and building owners to submit photographs, materials notes, and oral histories.

Structuring the archive so it can sit next to, and eventually inform, the Commission's own survey work.
